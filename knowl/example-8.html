Example 2.9.

Consider the Maclaurin series for \(e^x\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} e^x = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k!}x^k. \end{equation*}
Here
\begin{align*} \lim_{k\rightarrow\infty} \left| \frac{\frac{1}{(k+1)!}(x-a)^{k+1}}{\frac{1}{k!}(x-a)^k} \right| \amp = \lim_{k\to\infty}\left|\frac{k!}{(k+1)!} \right| \cdot|x| \\ \amp = \lim_{k\to\infty}\left|\frac{k!}{k!(k+1)} \right| \cdot|x| \\ \amp = \lim_{k\to\infty}\left|\frac{1}{k+1} \right| \cdot|x| \\ \amp = 0. \end{align*}
Thus the Maclaurin series for \(e^x\) converges for all values of \(x\text{.}\)
