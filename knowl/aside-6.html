We can actually do slightly better: in the Ratio Test we can replace \(\displaystyle L=\lim_{k\to \infty}\left|\dfrac{t_{k+1}}{t_k}\right|\) by \(\displaystyle L=\limsup_{k\to \infty}\left|\dfrac{t_{k+1}}{t_k}\right|\text{.}\)
If you donâ€™t yet know what a limsup is, here is another way of putting it: If there exists a number \(L \lt 1\text{,}\) such that \(\displaystyle \left|\dfrac{t_{k+1}}{t_k}\right| \leq L\) for all sufficiently large \(k\text{,}\) then the series converges.
The idea is simply that, in this case, the terms in the series shrink to zero faster than the terms of a (convergent) geometric series with common ratio \(L \lt 1\text{,}\) and thus it must also converge.
